Choosing an Enterprise Linux

August 22, 2013 trajano Leave a comment

Looking through some options on enterprise Linux. I find that I would likely recommend Oracle Linux over some of the choices out there.
My choices are driven by:

Commonality among different companies.

Vendor support.

Scalability of deployment

Scalability of skills

All your base are belong to us

One of the first things I looked at and conformed with colleagues working in large enterprises is to find out what distribution of Linux is used by their clients for the servers. What I found was all of the ones I had asked are RHEL based.

There isn’t anyone I hear using a Debian or SuSE based distribution.

So whatever i would recommend would be limited to RHEL based distributions. This eliminates Ubuntu which is Debian based.

Vendor support

The top 3 contenders that are RedHat Enterprise Linux, CentOS and Oracle Linux.

CentOS has no large vendor backing it as a product. So that alone will give me a bit of the chills if I put on my enterprise architect hat.

RedHat would male a hood choice, buy it isn’t available for free which means licensing issues that the enterprise needs to deal with when doing internal development testing.  Also RedHat is a much smaller company than that of Oracle.

That just leaves me with Oracle Linux. It is supported by a large company as a product.

Enterprise scale forward thinking

Two features that attracted me to Oracle Linux over others are

Amazon AWS support. Amazon is still the most popular cloud provider out there which large enterprises can outsource less critical infrastructure over to reduce internal costs.

IBM z series mainframe support. Large scale enterprises would likely have these machines which are pretty much disaster proof and battle tested over the years. Having support for this on that platform allows enterprises to scale up and down and retain skills on all levels.

Since it is free, development teams can roll out the servers with no procurement costs to do internal testing.

What about AIX?

Although you would have less integration issues with AIX since you’d be a full IBM stack, AIX isn’t free and will be difficult to work with by developers.

The cost of AIX administrators would be more expensive since they are a more specialized commodity than RHEL administrators.
